Digestzymes Ingredients

A line-by-line look at what's inside Designs for Health Digestzymes, including active components and excipients.

The full ingredient list is on the bottle and the brand's site. The short version is below — and it is a deliberately broad mix, with each piece handling a different part of your meal, while the betaine HCl puts back the stomach acid that starts it all.

Active Ingredients

What is actually in it:

  • Betaine HCl — the stomach acid; it restores your acidity and starts protein digestion, and it is the piece that sets this apart from a plain enzyme pill and goes after low acid at the root
  • Protease — breaks down protein
  • Lipase — breaks down fat; the one that helps most when a rich or fried meal sits heavy
  • Amylase — breaks down starch and other carbs
  • An enzyme with DPP-IV activity — helps break down the gluten and casein bits, which matters if you have a mild gluten or dairy sensitivity
  • Lactase and a few other enzymes — cover more of what is on your plate

Other Ingredients (Excipients)

Because it is a capsule and not a powder or a chewable, there is not really a taste question — the inactive list is short. Your current label is the place to look for the capsule material and any inactive ingredient, since they adjust it over time, so check there if you are sensitive to a particular additive. Honestly, the bigger thing with this one is when you take it, not what is in the capsule shell: take it as you start your meal, not after, so the acid and enzymes are ready when the food is.

Allergens and Sensitivities

The allergy notes here mostly come from the enzyme sources, and a rare reaction to one of them is what to keep an eye on. If you have a known sensitivity, check your current label — it carries the official allergen statement and should be the final word. Separately (and this is a stomach caution, not an allergy): the betaine HCl means anyone with an active ulcer or gastritis should steer clear of it entirely.

Sourcing and Quality Notes

Designs for Health is a US practitioner brand based in Connecticut that has been in the clinical world for decades and sells mostly through licensed providers and authorized distributors. They make to cGMP standards. Buying through the practitioner channel or their own store is the most reliable way to avoid the diverted or expired stock that sometimes shows up from random marketplace sellers — which matters even more for an enzyme product, since the enzymes only really work if the stock was handled properly and is not past its date. How Ingredients Compare to Similar Products

The main thing worth comparing is Digestzymes against a plain plant-only enzyme product. Both give you protease, lipase, and amylase, but Digestzymes adds betaine HCl for stomach acid — and that acid is really the whole point, because a lot of people with sluggish digestion are low on acid, not high, and a plant-only pill does nothing for that. The flip side is that the same acid is why Digestzymes is not for everyone: if you have an ulcer, gastritis, or a doctor-confirmed high-acid problem, a plain enzyme (or nothing) is the safer choice. Also good to know what it is not — it is not an antimicrobial and not a gut-lining repair product; it is a digestion helper. So the right pick depends on whether low stomach acid is part of your picture and whether an irritated stomach rules the acid out.
